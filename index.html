This scheme aimed to provide steady medical supports to millions of people, especially those with pre-existing conditions by encouraging young and healthy people to get their own insurance.

3. How did Obamacare help financially challenged Americans to afford health coverage?

This data was sourced from KFF. Key Facts about the Uninsured Population

Until the ACA, only half of financially challenged Americans were supported by Medicaid and almost 30% of those under the age of 65 with low-income did not have any coverage at all (Rice, T., Unruh, L. Y., van Ginneken, E., Rosenau, P., & Barnes, A. J. (2018)). After the ACA, the number of people lacking health insurance went from more than 46.5 million in 2010 to 18.9 million in 2019 .

The ACA increased individual coverage by:

  1. Prohibiting insurers from denying insurance or charging more for those with pre-existing conditions.
  2. Mandating everyone to get insurance
  3. Helping everyone to pay for their own insurance through income-based premium.

To date, it has provided coverage for more than 31 Million Americans!

4. What did not work?

The ACA was originally created so that all financially challenged Americans would receive Medicaid coverage, but that intent was hindered by the U.S. Supreme Court ruling.

The ACA did not achieve its goal for several reasons (Rice, T., Unruh, L. Y., van Ginneken, E., Rosenau, P., & Barnes, A. J. (2018)):

  1. Approximately 4.5 million of financially challenged Americans still do not receive Medicaid because most of them would need to spend more than 8% of their income on premiums.
  2. Some preferred to pay penalty than buying insurance because the penalty is milder compared to Germany, Netherlands and Switzerland.
  3. Undocumented individuals are forbidden from purchasing on the exchanges and are not eligible for subsidies.

5. Why does Trump hate Obamacare so much?

Although Obamacare has been well received by most Americans, Trump, however, is strongly against it. According to Stein, H. F., and Allcorn, S. (2018), it is believed that the root of hatred for Obama and Obamacare was originated from The annual Washington Post gala White House Correspondents’ Dinner on April 30, 2011, where Obama publicly "roasted" Trump.

Trump, who is easily ashamed, swore to destroy Obama and everything Obama did.

It is believed that after the incident at The annual Washington Post gala White House Correspondents’ Dinner, Trump became the most visible spokesperson for the "birther movement", which was believed to be an effort to delegitimize Obama as President.

6. What would happen if Trump were to repeal Obamacare?

If Obamacare were to be repealed, at least 12.3 million Americans would lose their insurance because it is not mandatory anymore (Saltzman, E., & Eibner, C. (2015)). This would cause an increase of 8% in market premiums in 2017 and even more today, especially during the pandemic. The increase of premiums would then cause significant coverage losses to many other Americans.

In short, by demolishing the ACA, millions of people would be "kicked" out of insurance. Losing insurance would mean losing health benefits and getting much higher medical costs.

The ACA, or Obamacare, has become so popular today than it was when it first passed in 2010. Yet, neither the Republicans nor Trump have proposed any alternative without ensuring the same protection for the people.

7. Conclusion

In conclusion, the ACA has helped not only providing subsidies to almost all Americans, but also slowing down the rise in medical costs. However, it can be viewed negatively by the fact that it mandates everyone to get their own insurance. This is also one of the reasons, besides the hatred for Obama, that Trump is so opposed to this Act.

Up until this date, Trump's effort to repeal Obamacare is still an ongoing process. Neither him nor his Republican party has yet to offer any alternative that could maintain the same coverage as Obamacare did for the Americans. This is very crucial because the need for insurance and medical coverage is highly important in 2020 and 2021, during the pandemic.
